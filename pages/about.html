About · Tentang · 关于

A digital humanities study of multilingual public signage in Kuala Lumpur— examining how Malay, Chinese, Tamil, English, and Jawi script coexist (or fail to) across 60 signs in 4 districts: Petaling Street, Little India, Bukit Bintang, and Kampung Baru.

Research Question

How does multilingual public signage in Kuala Lumpur reflect the layered tensions between state language policy and community linguistic practice?

Legal Background — Malaysia's Language Framework

Malaysia's National Language Act 1963/67 codifies Bahasa Melayu as the sole national language. The Federal Territory's Undang-Undang Kecil Iklan 1982 operationalizes this through Section 3, requiring all advertisements to be in the national language and giving Malay text typographic priority—larger in size than any other script.

Kuala Lumpur City Hall (DBKL) enforces a "30% rule": Malay text must occupy at least 30% of the signboard area, with prior approval from Dewan Bahasa dan Pustaka (DBP). In practice, this means every public sign is a small act of negotiation—between law, commerce, history, and identity.

Three-Layer Analytical Framework

We read the linguistic landscape through three interlocking layers:

MS · Bahasa Melayu
Top-down · Policy-driven
Mandated by national language law; appears on 91.7% of signs (55/60). Bilingual to compliance, not necessarily to community.
ZH · TA
Bottom-up · Community-driven
Chinese (46.7%) and Tamil (15%) appear where ethnic communities live and work. Their distribution mirrors district demographics, not policy.
EN · English
Economic Pragmatism
81.7% of signs include English—not mandated, not community-bound, but commercially universal. The lingua franca of consumption.

Project Contribution

Our contribution is not a single "headline finding" but a systematic framework + 8 patterns derived from 60 fieldwork samples:

  1. ZH–TA non-coexistence · 58/60 signs (96.7%); exceptions LI_07 (Ruddra Veenay music shop) + KB_01 (Klinik Kasut shoe repair)
  2. PS Chinese signs trilingual · 92.3% combine ZH+MS+EN
  3. KB monolingual island · 46.7% Malay-only; Jawi reserve (KB 3 / PS 2 / BB 1 / LI 0)
  4. Government 1.67 lang/sign vs Shop 2.66 · Commerce more multilingual than the state
  5. Tamil official 0% · across 5 government + 4 road signs
  6. Chinese radiation gradient · PS 86.7% > BB 46.7% > LI/KB 26.7%; Tamil concentrated in LI 46.7%
  7. Jawi/Arabic three functions · PS historical sediment / BB commercial internationalization / KB ethnic life heritage
  8. Religious-space exemption · only 3 signs use script freely outside the 30% rule

Findings 5–8 marked as sample-limited; conclusions tentative pending wider sampling.
Based on 60 signs across 4 districts.

Prior Literature

Our work extends David & Manan (2015), who examined 858 multilingual signs across Kuala Lumpur as a whole, treating the city as a mixed sample. Our contribution is the 4-district disaggregation: instead of city-wide averages, we compare how the same legal framework produces different linguistic landscapes when filtered through district demographics, history, and commerce.

Method

Sampling: 15 signs per district, randomly selected from public-facing commercial, residential, and government spaces. Total 4 × 15 = 60 signs.

Coding: Each sign was photographed and annotated with present languages, sign type, text content, and contextual notes. Data captured in a master Excel sheet with 13 fields per sign.

Analysis: Cross-district comparison via cluster maps and language co-occurrence matrices. Voyant Tools used for word-frequency exploration; TEI encoding tested on representative samples.
